Converting a van into a campervan is one of the most rewarding — and expensive — DIY projects you can take on. Van conversion costs in the UK typically range from £1,500 for a basic self-build to over £30,000 for a fully professional fit-out, depending on your van size, the features you include, and how much of the work you do yourself. This free tool helps you build a realistic budget before you spend a penny.

The average DIY campervan conversion takes around 445 hours and involves everything from insulation and electrics to bespoke cabinetry, solar panels, and plumbing. Costs vary significantly between a budget Chinese diesel heater (£100–300) and a branded Webasto, or between flatpack IKEA kitchen units and custom cabinetry — which is why this estimator lets you set both the feature and the quality tier for each line item.

Whether you're planning a weekend warrior build on a small Ford Transit Connect or a full-time off-grid home on a Mercedes Sprinter LWB, this tool covers 30 features across 8 skill-grouped categories — from basic preparation like rust treatment and sound deadening, through to solar power systems, gas hobs, water plumbing, and exterior accessories like Fiamma awnings and roof racks. ⚠ Legal requirements
  • Gas: legal for personal DIY if you're "competent" — for rental a Gas Safe engineer must sign off (BS EN 1949)
  • Electrics must meet BS7671. Get an auto-electrician inspection even on DIY wiring — insurers can reject claims otherwise
  • Travel seats must be crash-tested and fixed — homemade bench seats are not road-legal
  • CO alarm + smoke detector legally required wherever gas appliances are fitted
📋 DVLA reclassification
  • Optional but beneficial — only ~20% of applications approved even for perfect builds
  • Needs: fixed bed, permanent hob/microwave, seating+table, storage, side windows, awning rail, high-top (pop-tops excluded)
  • Benefits: lower insurance, higher speed limits (70mph dual carriageway), Class 4 MOT, cheaper ferry crossings
  • Reclassification is free via V5C — you may need to transport the van for DVLA inspection
🛡 Insurance & running costs
  • Specialist campervan insurance: £600–£2,000/yr
  • Campervans are typically 10–50% cheaper to insure than commercial vans
  • Notify your insurer before you start converting — standard van cover may already be void
  • Gas Safe certificate essential if you plan to rent the van out
⚖ Weight & licensing
  • Exceeding your van's MAM = fines + invalidated insurance
  • Over 3.5 tonnes requires a C1 licence beyond a standard UK driving licence
  • LWB Sprinter builds with full fit-out can easily approach the 3.5t limit
  • Get a weighbridge check once complete — costs ~£20 at most haulage depots

FAQ Common questions about van conversion costs
How much does a van conversion cost in the UK in 2025?▼
Van conversion costs in the UK range from around £1,500 for a very basic DIY build to over £30,000 for a fully professional fit-out. A realistic mid-range DIY conversion with insulation, electrics, solar, a kitchen and a fixed bed typically costs £4,000–£10,000 in parts for a medium-sized van like a Ford Transit. Professional conversion companies charge £10,000–£25,000 for a comparable build.
Is it cheaper to convert a van yourself?▼
Yes — significantly. DIY van conversions typically cost 40–60% less than hiring a professional company. A professional build that costs £15,000 might cost £6,000–£9,000 in materials if you do the labour yourself. The most cost-effective approach is a hybrid build: DIY the carpentry, insulation and fitting, then pay an auto-electrician and a Gas Safe engineer to inspect or install the technical systems.
How long does a van conversion take?▼
The average DIY van conversion takes approximately 445 hours of work from start to finish. Most part-time builders working weekends complete a full conversion in 3–6 months. Rushing is one of the biggest causes of costly mistakes — the cardboard mock-up stage, taking time to plan the layout and wiring routes before cutting anything, saves significant time and money later.
What is the most expensive part of a van conversion?▼
The electrical system is typically the most expensive single component, costing £700–£1,800 professionally installed and £300–£900 in parts for a competent DIY build. Lithium battery upgrades add another £400–£900 but are worth the cost for full-time use. Custom cabinetry and high-spec kitchens can also easily exceed £2,000–£4,000 at the professional end. On full professional builds, labour is usually the biggest cost overall.
Which van is best for a conversion?▼
The Mercedes Sprinter LWB and Ford Transit LWB are the most popular choices for full-time living builds — they offer maximum usable space (~4.3m load bay) and a huge community of converters providing advice and parts. For budget builds, the Ford Transit SWB or Fiat Ducato SWB offer an excellent balance of space, cost and reliability. Avoid older high-mileage Sprinters with the OM612 engine (known for expensive injector issues) unless you know the full service history.
Do I need a Gas Safe certificate for a van conversion?▼
You can legally install gas yourself for personal use in a UK van conversion, but you must be competent to do so and follow BS EN 1949. However, if you plan to rent the van out, a Gas Safe registered engineer must inspect and certify the gas system — this is a legal requirement. A gas safety certificate costs approximately £80–£150. Even if renting isn't planned, getting a sign-off is strongly recommended as uncertified gas systems can invalidate your insurance and create liability issues.
Should I register my converted van as a motor caravan with the DVLA?▼
Reclassification as a "motor caravan" is optional but has real benefits: lower insurance premiums (campervans are typically 10–50% cheaper to insure than commercial vans), higher speed limits (70mph on dual carriageways vs 60mph for vans), and potentially cheaper ferry fares. The process is free but notoriously difficult — only around 20% of applications are approved. Common rejection reasons are missing awning rails, no motor caravan graphics, or a pop-top roof (which the DVLA does not count as a high-top).
